# /plan-week -- PM Weekly Planning

Structure your week with clear priorities, time allocation, and risk flags. A practical planning routine that helps PMs stay proactive instead of reactive.

## Invocation

```
/plan-week Launching feature X next Thursday, have a board review Friday, and need to finalize Q3 OKRs
/plan-week I have 6 meetings tomorrow and I'm behind on two PRDs
/plan-week                    # starts with a review of your current situation
```

## Workflow

### Step 1: Review Last Week

Apply the **thought-partner** skill for reflection:

Start by looking back before looking forward. Ask:
- What did you accomplish last week? What shipped, what progressed, what stalled?
- What surprised you — interruptions, scope changes, new information?
- What carried over that still needs attention?
- Rate your week: Did you spend time on your highest-priority work, or did reactive tasks dominate?

```
## Last Week Review
- **Shipped/Completed**: [list]
- **In Progress**: [list with status]
- **Carried Over**: [list — these get priority consideration]
- **Surprises**: [what you didn't expect]
- **Reactive vs. Proactive ratio**: [estimate — aim for 60%+ proactive]
```

### Step 2: Identify This Week's Priorities

Separate what's urgent from what's important:

| Priority | Item | Type | Deadline | Impact |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| P0 | [must happen this week] | [ship/decide/unblock/communicate] | [date] | [what happens if missed] |
| P1 | [should happen this week] | [...] | [...] | [...] |
| P2 | [nice to complete] | [...] | [...] | [...] |

Rules:
- Maximum 3 P0 items — if everything is P0, nothing is
- Each item should have a clear "done" state
- Include both deliverables and relationship-building (1:1s, alignment conversations)

### Step 3: Allocate Time

Map priorities to your actual calendar:

```
## Time Allocation

### Monday
- [Morning]: [focus work — what specifically]
- [Afternoon]: [meetings + what to prepare]

### Tuesday
- [Morning]: [...]
- [Afternoon]: [...]

### Wednesday - Friday
[Continue pattern]

### Buffer time
[At least 20% of your week should be unscheduled for reactive work and thinking time]
```

Guidelines:
- Protect 2-3 morning blocks for deep work (writing, strategy, analysis)
- Batch meetings where possible — context-switching is the PM productivity killer
- Schedule the hardest thinking work for your peak energy time
- Leave Friday afternoon for reflection and next-week prep

### Step 4: Flag Risks and Dependencies

Identify what could derail your week:

```
## Risks
- [Risk 1]: [mitigation or contingency]
- [Risk 2]: [mitigation or contingency]

## Dependencies
- [Waiting on X from Y — follow up by Wednesday if no response]
- [Need decision from Z before starting W]

## If Things Go Sideways
[If a P0 item blows up, which P1 items get pushed? Pre-decide so you're not scrambling]
```

### Step 5: Weekly Output

Compile into a clean weekly plan:

```
## Week of [Date]

### Top 3 Priorities
1. [P0 — what and why]
2. [P0 — what and why]
3. [P0 — what and why]

### Key Meetings & Prep Needed
- [Meeting] — [what to prepare, what outcome you want]

### Deep Work Blocks
- [When] — [what you'll work on]

### Risks & Dependencies
- [Key risks and mitigations]

### End-of-Week Success Criteria
[How will you know this was a good week?]
```

## Notes

- Run this Sunday evening or Monday morning for best results
- A good weekly plan is a living document — update it Wednesday when reality changes
- If you're spending more than 30 minutes on weekly planning, you're overcomplicating it
- The reactive vs. proactive ratio is the most important metric to track over time
- Share your weekly priorities with your manager — it creates alignment and protects your time
